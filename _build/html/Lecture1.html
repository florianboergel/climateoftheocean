Introduction to the course Climate of the ocean#

This lecture deals with fundamental processes in the climate system of the atmosphere, ocean, and sea ice. Basic methods of analysis and modeling of the climate system with focus on the ocean, equations of motion of the large-scale circulation, coupled climate models for atmosphere, ocean and sea ice and spatial and temporal variability of the large-scale circulation and the climate system will be discussed.

Literature recommendations:\

  • Ulrich Cubasch: Lecture notes

  • Dietmar Dommenget: Lecture notes

  • James R. Holton: An introduction to dynamic meterology

  • Geoffrey K. Vallis: Climate and the oceans

  • Geoffrey K.Vallis: Atmospheric and oceanic fluid dynamics

Basic Conservation Laws#

Fundamental forces#

A fluid element is an infinitesimal, indivisible piece of fluid. In the following, some assumptions are used:

  • It contains a large number of molecules to ignore the dynamics of individual molecules.

  • It is small compared to the volume of the atmosphere under consideration (hence all parts of the element are affected by the same forces).

Looking at the forces affecting the fluid parcel we use Newton’s second law: $\(m \vec{a} = \sum_i \vec{F_i}\)\( with the acceleration \)\vec{a}\(. The forces \)\vec{F_i}$ in the inertial system are classified as body forces (e.g. gravitation), surface forces (e.g. friction, pressure gradient) and apparent forces in rotating coordinate systems (e.g. Coriolis, centrifugal force).

Gravitational force#

{width=”35%”}

With the gravitational constant \(G\) and the distance \(r = a+z\) the gravitational force can be described as:

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \vec{F_g} = - \frac{G M m}{r^2} \left(\frac{\vec{r}}{r}\right) \\ \vec{g} ^{\ast} \equiv \frac{\vec{F_{g}}}{m} = - \frac{G M}{a^2 (1 + \frac{z}{a})^2} \left(\frac{\vec{r}}{r}\right) \end{aligned}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{aligned} \text{with the approximation} \ z \ll a (\text{height of the troposphere about 10 km, a = } 6.37\cdot 10^6m)\end{aligned}\]
\[\boxed{\vec{g} ^{\ast} \approx \vec{g_0} ^{\ast}\equiv -\frac{G M}{a^2} \left(\frac{\vec{r}}{r}\right) }\]

\(\rightarrow\) gravitational acceleration is constant
